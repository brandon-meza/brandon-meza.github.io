About

Making the molecular world interpretable

I am a computational chemist. My work sits at the meeting point of two skills that rarely live together: resolving how molecules actually behave through physics-based simulation, and making that behavior interpretable with data-driven analysis.

I am not satisfied by models that predict a number without explaining it — a prediction without a mechanism cannot become a design rule. So I build methods that connect molecular-scale detail to causal, explainable insight, and I apply them across a deliberately broad range of systems: biomaterials and membranes, reaction mechanisms and catalysis, molecular sensing, and nanomaterials.

I work hand-in-hand with experimentalists — most of my papers pair simulation with experiment, in membranes, molecular sensing, and materials synthesis. Trained in Mexico, Switzerland, and Japan, I now lead this line of research independently as a Program-Specific Assistant Professor at Kyoto University.

Research

What I work on

Transport

Molecular transport in soft & biomaterials

How molecules move through nanopores and membranes. In recent work I helped uncover a "pepper-mill" collective motion that drives DNA transport through a protein nanopore — a mechanism invisible to static pictures.

Interpretable AI

Data-driven, interpretable chemistry

I led an open dataset of quantum-topological atomic properties for ~44,000 molecules — built to link molecular structure to explainable predictions and to feed force fields and machine-learning models.

Mechanism

Reaction mechanisms & catalysis

Using enhanced-sampling simulations (metadynamics, ab initio MD) to reconstruct how reactions actually proceed — for example, how a copper complex catalyzes the hydrolysis of phosphate esters.

Materials

Functional molecules, sensing & nanomaterials

From naked-eye molecular sensors to biomaterials for water remediation and hydrated metal nanoclusters — understanding and designing functional behavior from the bottom up.

An open dataset for interpretable AI

I led Quantum Topological Atomic Properties of 44K Molecules (Scientific Data, 2024) — an openly released dataset of atomic energies, populations, and dipole/quadrupole moments computed with the Quantum Theory of Atoms in Molecules. It is designed to train interpretable machine-learning models that predict reactivity and properties directly from structure, and to feed next-generation force fields.

From mechanism to design

Whether the system is a membrane, a catalyst, or a sensor, the same question drives me: can we move from observing useful behavior to engineering it? Mapping the hidden energy landscapes and collective motions behind a function is the first step toward designing the next one.
